Turtle Games – Loyalty & Segmentation Analysis

Predictive modelling, segmentation, and sentiment analysis on 2,000 customers and reviews.
This project was completed as part of the LSE DA301: Advanced Analytics for Business course.

Final Score: Distinction

Executive Summary

Why this matters

  • Spending score is the strongest driver of loyalty (β ≈ 34).
  • Mid-income customers (£50–70k) show the highest loyalty engagement.
  • Income effect plateaus after £70k → diminishing returns on loyalty.
  • Sentiment analysis shows mostly positive reviews (avg polarity +0.12) but flags issues with rules and product quality.

Board-level actions:

  1. Target engaged spenders with bonus multipliers, regardless of income.
  2. Focus loyalty budget on the £50–70k “sweet spot” segment.
  3. Leverage reviews to promote joy/gifting while addressing negative themes like rules complexity.

Tools & Techniques

Languages & Libraries: Python (pandas, seaborn, scikit-learn, TextBlob), R (ggplot2, caret)

Techniques Applied:

  • Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) and KPI dashboards
  • Multiple Linear Regression (R² = 0.84)
  • Decision Tree (R² = 0.94) for loyalty prediction
  • Logistic Regression (AUC ≈ 0.99) to identify high-value customers
  • K-means clustering → 3 clear customer segments
  • Sentiment analysis and Named Entity Recognition on 2,000+ reviews

Highlights & Visuals

Regression: Predicting Loyalty Points

The regression explains 84% of loyalty point variation. Spending score dominates as a predictor, with income tapering off after ~£70k.

Customer Segmentation (K-means)

Three segments emerged: (1) high-income/high-spend VIPs, (2) mid-income core spenders, (3) high-income low-spenders. Behavioural engagement, not demographics, drives value.

Customer Sentiment

Positive themes dominate (“fun”, “great”, “love”), while negative mentions highlight rule complexity and quality concerns. Popular games include D&D, UNO, and Lords of Waterdeep.
